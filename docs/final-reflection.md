# Final Reflection Report

## Professional Engagement

Presenting Could've Been Worse at the Joburg Game Dev Meetup revealed the gap between academic and public critique. Academic assessment was against a rubric: does the LLM work? Is the architecture sound? At a public meetup, none of that mattered. Attendees cared about one thing: did the game feel good to play?

This difference was striking. The assessor asked about multi-model architecture. The indie developer asked about a unique hook. The hobbyist asked why the background was hard to read. Public feedback was immediate, experiential, and unconcerned with the technical scaffolding I had spent months building. Presenting work publicly forces you to see it through the eyes of someone who will never read your design document — if the surface is cluttered, that is the entire game to them.

I also learned the importance of a strong initial pitch. In a room of developers showing passion projects, a 15-second description of an AI-generated intelligence desk simulation did not generate the same energy as neighbouring games. The LLM was not a selling point to people who make games by hand. I need to pitch the player experience first and let the technology be a footnote.

## Feedback Integration

The indie developer's observation that the game lacked a consistent pull highlighted that procedural variety is not the same as a reason to keep playing. While re-architecting around a pre-authored narrative was declined as scope-breaking, the insight drove narrative recap injection to maintain context across rounds.

The hobbyist's three priority items — softlock bug, report clutter, and text clipping — were fixed immediately. Later playtesting revealed further issues: cascading validation failures and quality overseer model refusals. These drove the intercept/reply pipeline split, scenario parsing resilience with fallback values, and overseer hardening with expanded refusal and chatty-output detection.

The academic feedback about multi-model architecture proved more implementable than initially assumed. Each prompt type now routes to a dedicated model sequentially rather than concurrently, with an optional quality overseer for second-pass polish. Both the multi-model routing and the Bug Squash minigame, then built after the overseer added latency. This all directly address the assessor's requests for more AI control and reduced perceived wait time, while preserving the performance constraint.

## Collaboration With AI

The public playtest indirectly reshaped how I think about prompt engineering. The takeaway was not "write better prompts" but "the LLM output is only as good as the scaffolding around it." The softlock bug, for instance, was not an LLM problem, it was a state management problem in the Unity controller. The background clutter was not a generation problem: it was a UI layering problem. The feedback pushed me to focus on the game systems that wrap around the LLM rather than endlessly refining the prompts themselves.

This has implications for future AI-integrated projects. External critique of an AI-driven feature often reveals problems in the human-built infrastructure around that feature. A player complaining about too much reading is not complaining about the LLM — they are complaining about pacing, layout, and information density, all of which are design decisions I made, not the model.

## Ethical and Professional Considerations

Presenting a heavily AI-assisted game at a meetup dedicated to human craftsmanship was genuinely uncomfortable. I felt tension between the academic brief (which required LLM integration) and cultural values of the room (which celebrated hand-made work). I was transparent about the AI's role — Ollama generated scenario text, intercepts, and replies locally — but transparency did not resolve the discomfort. It only made the tension explicit.

This reshapes how I will approach disclosure. Transparency about AI use is necessary but insufficient. What matters more is how you frame the human contribution. I will lead with what I designed and built — the state machine, validation pipeline, scoring system, satirical tone and present the LLM as a tool serving those human-authored systems, not as a co-author. The ethical challenge is to ensure human creative decisions remain visible and valued.

Ownership of LLM-generated content remains grey. The intercepts and replies in Could've Been Worse were generated by llama3.1:8b locally, but shaped by hand-crafted prompts, constrained by hand-written validation rules, and scored by human designed logic. The product as a whole is mine. In a professional context, I would make this chain of authorship explicit: the model generates raw text, but the designer owns the rules, filters, tone, and experience.

The most important professional lesson from this process is that critique is not a verdict on your work. It is rahter a gift of perspective. Processing honest, unfiltered feedback, and integrating only what aligns with your design goals, is a skill that will outlast any single project. The ability to listen, filter, and refine does not.
