# Final Reflection Report

## Professional Engagement

Presenting Could've Been Worse at the Joburg Game Dev Meetup revealed the gap between academic and public critique. Academics assess against a rubric: does the LLM work? Is the architecture sound? At a public meetup, none of that mattered. Attendees cared about one thing: did the game feel good to play?

This difference was striking. The assessor asked about multi-model architecture. The indie developer asked about a unique hook. The hobbyist asked why the background was hard to read. Nobody asked about prompt engineering, validation pipelines, or model selection. Public feedback was immediate, experiential, and unconcerned with the technical scaffolding I had spent months building. Presenting work publicly forces you to see it through the eyes of someone who will never read your design document — if the surface is cluttered, that is the entire game to them.

I also learned the importance of a strong initial pitch. In a room of developers showing passion projects, a 15-second description of an AI-generated intelligence desk simulation did not generate the same energy as neighbouring games. The LLM was not a selling point to people who make games by hand. I need to pitch the player experience first and let the technology be a footnote.

## Feedback Integration

The most valuable feedback came from the indie developer. His observation that the game lacked a unique, consistent pull pointed to a structural issue: procedural variety is not the same as a reason to keep playing. His suggestion to look at Stories Untold helped me see that the project's biggest strength (procedural unpredictability) was also its biggest weakness (narrative drift).

The hobbyist's feedback was equally actionable. The report screen clutter and text clipping were problems I was vaguely aware of but had deprioritised. These became the three implemented refinements: the softlock guard, the readability plate, and the overflow fix.

The academic feedback about context retention and multi-model architecture was thoughtful but suggested a different project. It did validate that maintaining narrative coherence across procedural generation is a recognised challenge worth solving.

## Collaboration With AI

The public playtest indirectly reshaped how I think about prompt engineering. The takeaway was not "write better prompts" but "the LLM output is only as good as the scaffolding around it." The softlock bug, for instance, was not an LLM problem — it was a state management problem in the Unity controller. The background clutter was not a generation problem — it was a UI layering problem. The feedback pushed me to focus on the game systems that wrap around the LLM rather than endlessly refining the prompts themselves.

This has implications for future AI-integrated projects. External critique of an AI-driven feature often reveals problems in the human-built infrastructure around that feature. A player complaining about too much reading is not complaining about the LLM — they are complaining about pacing, layout, and information density, all of which are design decisions I made, not the model.

## Ethical and Professional Considerations

Presenting a heavily AI-assisted game at a meetup dedicated to human craftsmanship was genuinely uncomfortable. I felt tension between the academic brief (which required LLM integration) and cultural values of the room (which celebrated hand-made work). I was transparent about the AI's role — Ollama generated scenario text, intercepts, and replies locally — but transparency did not resolve the discomfort. It only made the tension explicit.

This reshapes how I will approach disclosure. Transparency about AI use is necessary but insufficient. What matters more is how you frame the human contribution. I will lead with what I designed and built — the state machine, validation pipeline, scoring system, satirical tone — and present the LLM as a tool serving those human-authored systems, not as a co-author. The ethical challenge is to ensure human creative decisions remain visible and valued.

Ownership of LLM-generated content remains grey. The intercepts and replies in Could've Been Worse were generated by llama3.1:8b locally, but shaped by hand-crafted prompts, constrained by hand-written validation rules, and scored by hand-designed logic. The product as a whole is mine. In a professional context, I would make this chain of authorship explicit: the model generates raw text, but the designer owns the rules, filters, tone, and experience.

The most important professional lesson from this entire process is that critique is not a verdict on your work — it is a gift of someone else's perspective. Receiving honest, unfiltered feedback from strangers at a meetup, processing it critically, and integrating only what aligns with your design goals is a skill that will outlast any single project. The AI industry moves fast. The ability to listen, filter, and refine does not.
